For Business Owners & the Self-Employed

You Built the Business.
Don't Let the IRS
Take Half of What You Saved.

Cash balance plans are one of the most powerful retirement savings tools available to business owners. Here's the part most people don't plan for — and how to fix it before you exit.

By Jeffrey Faine, CLU  ·  Integrated Planning Partners  ·  integratedplanningpartners.com

Start Here

The Tool That Works Almost Too Well

If you're a business owner or self-employed professional, a cash balance plan may be the single most efficient retirement savings vehicle available to you. The contribution limits dwarf a standard 401(k). The tax deductions are immediate and significant. And for high earners who started saving late — or who want to accelerate retirement savings in the final decade of their career — there's nothing else that moves the needle as quickly.

Here's how it works in plain terms: a cash balance plan is a type of defined benefit pension plan. Each year, you contribute a set amount to the plan — often well above $100,000, and in some cases over $300,000 or more depending on your age and income — and those contributions are fully deductible as a business expense. The money grows in the plan, and when you eventually exit the business or retire, it becomes available for distribution.

That last part — distribution — is where the conversation gets more complicated.

A cash balance plan is extraordinary at building wealth tax-deferred. It was not designed to distribute that wealth tax-efficiently. That's a problem you have to solve separately.

The same feature that makes it powerful on the way in — pre-tax contributions and tax-deferred growth — creates a substantial tax liability on the way out. Every dollar that comes out of a cash balance plan at retirement is taxed as ordinary income. For a business owner with $1 million or more accumulated, that can mean handing hundreds of thousands of dollars to the IRS at exactly the moment you needed that money most.

Understanding the Vehicle

Cash Balance vs. Traditional 401(k):
The Key Differences

Both are qualified retirement plans, which means both are pre-tax, both grow tax-deferred, and both are fully taxable on distribution. The distinction that matters for business owners is scale.

401(k) / Profit Sharing Cash Balance Plan
Annual contribution limit (age 60) ~$70,000 $250,000–$330,000+
Tax deduction Immediate, business expense Immediate, business expense
Growth Tax-deferred (market-linked) Tax-deferred (guaranteed credit rate)
Distribution tax treatment Ordinary income — 100% Ordinary income — 100%
RMD requirement Age 73 Age 73
Typical accumulation (10-yr window, high earner) $700K–$900K $2M–$3.5M+

The cash balance plan wins decisively on accumulation. A business owner who maximizes contributions for 10 years can realistically build $2 million to $3.5 million in pre-tax retirement assets — three to four times what a 401(k) alone would produce. That's the tool working exactly as designed.

But both plans share the same distribution problem. When that money comes out, ordinary income tax applies to every dollar. And here's what that means in practice: if you need $80,000 to cover a year of living expenses, you don't withdraw $80,000 — you may need to withdraw $110,000 to $115,000 to net that amount after federal and state income taxes. That gap between what you pull out and what you actually spend is the hidden cost of a pre-tax account. Multiplied across every withdrawal for 20 or 25 years of retirement, it can quietly consume $400,000 to $700,000 or more — money you earned, saved, and deferred taxes on, handed to the IRS one distribution at a time.

The Transition Risk

When the Business Exits,
the Tax Bill Arrives

For most business owners, the cash balance plan isn't just a retirement account — it's part of the exit strategy. When you sell the business, wind down operations, or transition to a successor, the plan balance becomes accessible. And that's when the tax exposure becomes real and urgent.

A Common Scenario

A physician in solo practice has contributed aggressively to a cash balance plan for 12 years. At exit, the plan balance is $2.4 million. Combined with a 401(k) and other qualified assets, total pre-tax retirement savings approach $3 million.

She rolls the cash balance plan into an IRA at exit — a clean, tax-free rollover. But now she has $3 million of pre-tax wealth that will be taxed at ordinary income rates every time she touches it. When she needs $10,000 for a home renovation, she doesn't withdraw $10,000 — she withdraws $13,500 to $14,500 to net that amount after federal and state taxes. The $3,500 to $4,500 difference goes to the IRS. Multiplied across 20 to 25 years of retirement, that gross-up tax drag quietly erodes hundreds of thousands of dollars she saved over a career.

At 73, Required Minimum Distributions will force withdrawals she may not need, stacking taxable income year after year — whether she wants the money or not.

The plan worked perfectly. The distribution strategy was never built.

The RMD Gross-Up Problem

At age 73, Required Minimum Distributions on a $2.5 million IRA (after assumed growth) may exceed $100,000 per year — mandatory, taxable income regardless of what else you're earning. But the gross-up math compounds the problem further: to fund $80,000 of actual spending from that RMD, you may need $110,000 to $120,000 in gross distributions once federal taxes, state taxes, and IRMAA Medicare surcharges are accounted for.

The problem isn't that you have too much saved. The problem is that all of it is in one tax bucket — and every dollar you spend from that bucket costs you meaningfully more than a dollar to access.

The Opportunity

The Window Most Business Owners
Don't Know They Have

Here is what changes the picture entirely: the years surrounding a business exit — typically ages 55 to 68 — are often the single best window for a business owner to address their qualified plan tax liability. And most people never act on it, because they don't know a structured solution exists.

Before the business sells, income may be at its peak, which feels like a reason to wait. After the sale, there may be a period of lower income, which creates a genuine opportunity to address pre-tax assets at favorable rates. But in both cases, the constraint is the same: the tax bill is large, liquid assets are often tied up in the transaction, and the conventional tools — gradual conversions, ladder strategies — aren't built for balances of this size.

The business exit creates a window. The question is whether you have a structure to take advantage of it — or whether you're simply rolling a large tax problem from one account into another.

  • The gap year opportunity. In the year or two between business exit and new income streams beginning, taxable income often drops significantly. That compressed window is ideal for addressing the tax liability — if the right structure is in place before that window opens.

  • Age is a factor. The strategy that makes this work is most efficient when initiated in the mid-to-late 50s and early 60s. A business owner who acts before 65 has access to meaningfully better economics than one who waits. The window doesn't close, but it narrows.

  • Pre-RMD runway. Every year before age 73 is a year you control your own distribution schedule. A structured strategy executed in this window can eliminate mandatory withdrawals before they ever begin — on your terms, not the IRS's.

  • Estate and succession clarity. A large pre-tax qualified plan is one of the most tax-inefficient assets to pass to heirs. Addressing the tax liability now — rather than deferring it into your estate — protects the legacy you spent decades building.

The Honest Truth

Why Most Business Owners
Never Act on It

Everything above is true. And yet the majority of business owners who recognize this problem — and genuinely intend to address it — never act at the scale that would actually change the outcome. The reason is not a lack of willpower. It's a cash flow problem.

Addressing the tax liability on a qualified plan doesn't make the taxes disappear. It moves them forward. You pay them now, on the amount distributed, at ordinary income rates. That means if you have $2 million in a cash balance plan rolled into an IRA, you're looking at a tax bill that could easily reach $600,000 to $800,000 or more.

You can withhold that tax directly from the account — but doing so means a significant portion of your balance never works for you again. If you distribute $2 million and withhold $640,000 to cover the taxes, that $640,000 is gone permanently. It will never compound. It will never generate income. It will never pass to your heirs. The money that paid the tax bill disappears from your financial picture entirely.

The mathematically superior approach — and the one that actually maximizes the outcome — is to pay the tax from assets outside the account, so the full balance can be repositioned intact. For most business owners at exit, that outside cash simply doesn't exist in the amounts required. Liquid assets are committed to the transaction, reinvested, or not yet accessible. And that is precisely where the strategy breaks down.

The problem isn't the idea. The problem is that most people try to retire a large debt with tools designed for a small one.

This is the gap that most exit planning conversations never reach. The strategy is correct. The execution is where it breaks down — and where a structured approach changes everything.

A Different Approach

What Changes When the
Tax Bill Is Financed

Remember what we established earlier: the tax on your qualified plan isn't a future event — it's an existing debt. The government already holds a lien on a significant portion of everything you've saved. That lien doesn't disappear at exit. It gets paid one way or another, on terms that are largely set by someone else.

With IncomeArc™, we do one better — we pay the tax from outside the account using the bank's money.

What IncomeArc™ does is refinance that debt. Instead of leaving the government's lien in place — accruing silently, subject to whatever tax rates Congress sets, forced into repayment on the IRS's schedule at 73 — we replace it with bank financing on terms you control. The outstanding tax obligation owed to the government is retired using the bank's capital, not yours.

And here is what most people don't expect when they hear the word "bank": there is no personal guaranty. The loan is secured solely by the life insurance policy itself. Your home, your other accounts, your liquid assets from the sale — none of them are on the hook. The liability hasn't changed in size since it was owed to the government. What changed are two things that matter enormously: the timeline, and the rate.

The government's effective rate on this debt is your marginal income tax bracket — 24%, 32%, 37%, or higher, depending on your situation, and subject to increase any time Congress chooses. The bank's rate is approximately 5% to 6%. That is the refinancing. You are trading a 24% to 40% obligation on the government's repayment schedule for a 5% to 6% obligation on yours. In any other context — a mortgage, a business loan, a line of credit — that would be an obvious decision. Applied to the largest debt most business owners are carrying without fully recognizing it, it changes the entire picture of what exit can look like.

Your interest rate will never be as high as your tax rate. And your tax rate will never be as low as your interest rate.

But the refinancing doesn't stop with the bank. As the policy accumulates cash value over time, the next step is to refinance again — this time replacing the bank loan with a policy loan from the insurance company. This is simply changing the lender one more time. The bank is paid off. The insurance company becomes the new creditor at typically even more favorable terms.

This step matters for a specific reason: the bank must be repaid before you can begin drawing tax-free income from the policy. Once the bank is retired and the policy loan is in place, the cash value inside the IUL becomes accessible as tax-free income — drawn against the policy's accumulated value without triggering a taxable event. That is the income the policy was built to produce.

From that point forward, the remaining policy loan — now held by the insurance company, not the bank — simply rides. There is no required repayment schedule. No personal guaranty. No forced distribution. When you pass, the gross death benefit repays the outstanding policy loan in full. What remains — the net death benefit — passes to your beneficiaries completely tax-free.

Consider what that means compared to the alternative. An inherited cash balance plan or traditional IRA comes with the full tax liability attached. Your beneficiaries are required to draw it down within ten years and pay ordinary income tax on every dollar. What you intended as a legacy arrives as a tax bill with your name on it. What the IncomeArc™ structure leaves them is a tax-free inheritance, after a debt that began as a government lien quietly retired itself from within the policy.

Here's what the bank financing makes possible. Each layer of the IncomeArc™ structure does its own independent job — and they are designed to work together for a better combined outcome than either produces alone:

Layer 3 — Tax Efficiency and long-term growth. The distribution proceeds fund an Indexed Universal Life policy — accumulating cash value with tax-deferred growth, protected from market downside, and accessible tax-free. The bank loan services the tax obligation on the distribution. Your personal savings stay intact. Over time, the IUL grows, the bank is refinanced through a policy loan, and the cash value becomes the source of additional tax-free income from Layer 3.

Layer 1 — The Guaranteed Income Foundation. The converted Roth balance funds a guaranteed annuity — contractual, permanent, completely tax-free income for life. This is Layer 1, and it can be funded independently of Layer 3. What Layer 3 does is remove the cashflow friction that limits how much gets funded. Without the leverage component, a significant portion of the conversion is consumed by the tax bill. With it, far more of the converted balance reaches Layer 1 intact — which means a larger, more complete guaranteed income foundation.

This is the competitive advantage of the IncomeArc™ system. The architecture itself — four layers, each doing a distinct job — is more complete than what most exit planning delivers. But what takes it to the next level is the leverage: bringing bank financing into the structure removes the cashflow drag of taxes and allows the system to work at a scale that conventional planning simply cannot reach.

The business is behind you. The income is in front of you — guaranteed, structured, and permanently outside the reach of the IRS. Layer 1 is built to fund as much of your Life Rate™ as possible. Everything else is upside.

Business owners are accustomed to thinking in terms of revenue, margin, and cash flow. In retirement, the equivalent question is simple: what does it cost to run your life?

We call that your Life Rate™. It captures the full picture — not just the mortgage and utilities, but the travel, the family occasions, the philanthropy, the lifestyle that three or four decades of building a business was supposed to make possible. Targeting that number with income that is guaranteed and tax-free is the foundation of a retirement that actually works.

The IncomeArc™ architecture is built to target your Life Rate™ from Layer 1 — the Guaranteed Income Foundation — using income that is contractual, permanent, and designed to be as insulated as possible from the variables that make retirement planning feel uncertain.

For business owners who spent decades writing large checks to the IRS and managing the tax complexity of running a company, there's something clarifying about arriving at retirement with a Layer 1 income stream that cannot be taxed, cannot be forced, and is not subject to market performance. That's what the structure is designed to produce.
