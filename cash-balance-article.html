Cash balance plans are one of the most powerful retirement savings tools available to business owners. Here's the part most people don't plan for â and how to fix it before you exit.

The Tool That Works Almost Too Well

If you're a business owner or self-employed professional, a cash balance plan may be the single most efficient retirement savings vehicle available to you. The contribution limits dwarf a standard 401(k). The tax deductions are immediate and significant. And for high earners who started saving late â or who want to accelerate retirement savings in the final decade of their career â there's nothing else that moves the needle as quickly.

Here's how it works in plain terms: a cash balance plan is a type of defined benefit pension plan. Each year, you contribute a set amount to the plan â often well above $100,000, and in some cases over $300,000 or more depending on your age and income â and those contributions are fully deductible as a business expense. The money grows in the plan, and when you eventually exit the business or retire, it becomes available for distribution.

That last part â distribution â is where the conversation gets more complicated.

A cash balance plan is extraordinary at building wealth tax-deferred. It was not designed to distribute that wealth tax-efficiently. That's a problem you have to solve separately.

The same feature that makes it powerful on the way in â pre-tax contributions and tax-deferred growth â creates a substantial tax liability on the way out. Every dollar that comes out of a cash balance plan at retirement is taxed as ordinary income. For a business owner with $1 million or more accumulated, that can mean handing hundreds of thousands of dollars to the IRS at exactly the moment you needed that money most.

The cash balance plan wins decisively on accumulation. A business owner who maximizes contributions for 10 years can realistically build $2 million to $3.5 million in pre-tax retirement assets â three to four times what a 401(k) alone would produce. That's the tool working exactly as designed.

But both plans share the same distribution problem. When that money comes out, ordinary income tax applies to every dollar. And here's what that means in practice: if you need $80,000 to cover a year of living expenses, you don't withdraw $80,000 â you may need to withdraw $110,000 to $115,000 to net that amount after federal and state income taxes. That gap between what you pull out and what you actually spend is the hidden cost of a pre-tax account. Multiplied across every withdrawal for 20 or 25 years of retirement, it can quietly consume $400,000 to $700,000 or more â money you earned, saved, and deferred taxes on, handed to the IRS one distribution at a time.

The Transition Risk

When the Business Exits,
the Tax Bill Arrives

For most business owners, the cash balance plan isn't just a retirement account â it's part of the exit strategy. When you sell the business, wind down operations, or transition to a successor, the plan balance becomes accessible. And that's when the tax exposure becomes real and urgent.

A Common Scenario

A physician in solo practice has contributed aggressively to a cash balance plan for 12 years. At exit, the plan balance is $2.4 million. Combined with a 401(k) and other qualified assets, total pre-tax retirement savings approach $3 million.

She rolls the cash balance plan into an IRA at exit â a clean, tax-free rollover. But now she has $3 million of pre-tax wealth that will be taxed at ordinary income rates every time she touches it. When she needs $10,000 for a home renovation, she doesn't withdraw $10,000 â she withdraws $13,500 to $14,500 to net that amount after federal and state taxes. The $3,500 to $4,500 difference goes to the IRS. Multiplied across 20 to 25 years of retirement, that gross-up tax drag quietly erodes hundreds of thousands of dollars she saved over a career.

At 73, Required Minimum Distributions will force withdrawals she may not need, stacking taxable income year after year â whether she wants the money or not.

The plan worked perfectly. The distribution strategy was never built.

The RMD Gross-Up Problem

At age 73, Required Minimum Distributions on a $2.5 million IRA (after assumed growth) may exceed $100,000 per year â mandatory, taxable income regardless of what else you're earning. But the gross-up math compounds the problem further: to fund $80,000 of actual spending from that RMD, you may need $110,000 to $120,000 in gross distributions once federal taxes, state taxes, and IRMAA Medicare surcharges are accounted for.

The problem isn't that you have too much saved. The problem is that all of it is in one tax bucket â and every dollar you spend from that bucket costs you meaningfully more than a dollar to access.

The Opportunity

The Window Most Business Owners
Don't Know They Have

Here is what changes the picture entirely: the years surrounding a business exit â typically ages 55 to 68 â are often the single best window for a business owner to address their qualified plan tax liability. And most people never act on it, because they don't know a structured solution exists.

Before the business sells, income may be at its peak, which feels like a reason to wait. After the sale, there may be a period of lower income, which creates a genuine opportunity to address pre-tax assets at favorable rates. But in both cases, the constraint is the same: the tax bill is large, liquid assets are often tied up in the transaction, and the conventional tools â gradual conversions, ladder strategies â aren't built for balances of this size.

The business exit creates a window. The question is whether you have a structure to take advantage of it â or whether you're simply rolling a large tax problem from one account into another.

  • The gap year opportunity. In the year or two between business exit and new income streams beginning, taxable income often drops significantly. That compressed window is ideal for addressing the tax liability â if the right structure is in place before that window opens.

  • Age is a factor. The strategy that makes this work is most efficient when initiated in the mid-to-late 50s and early 60s. A business owner who acts before 65 has access to meaningfully better economics than one who waits. The window doesn't close, but it narrows.

  • Pre-RMD runway. Every year before age 73 is a year you control your own distribution schedule. A structured strategy executed in this window can eliminate mandatory withdrawals before they ever begin â on your terms, not the IRS's.

  • Estate and succession clarity. A large pre-tax qualified plan is one of the most tax-inefficient assets to pass to heirs. Addressing the tax liability now â rather than deferring it into your estate â protects the legacy you spent decades building.

The Honest Truth

Why Most Business Owners
Never Act on It

Everything above is true. And yet the majority of business owners who recognize this problem â and genuinely intend to address it â never act at the scale that would actually change the outcome. The reason is not a lack of willpower. It's a cash flow problem.

Addressing the tax liability on a qualified plan doesn't make the taxes disappear. It moves them forward. You pay them now, on the amount distributed, at ordinary income rates. That means if you have $2 million in a cash balance plan rolled into an IRA, you're looking at a tax bill that could easily reach $600,000 to $800,000 or more.

You can withhold that tax directly from the account â but doing so means a significant portion of your balance never works for you again. If you distribute $2 million and withhold $640,000 to cover the taxes, that $640,000 is gone permanently. It will never compound. It will never generate income. It will never pass to your heirs. The money that paid the tax bill disappears from your financial picture entirely.

The mathematically superior approach â and the one that actually maximizes the outcome â is to pay the tax from assets outside the account, so the full balance can be repositioned intact. For most business owners at exit, that outside cash simply doesn't exist in the amounts required. Liquid assets are committed to the transaction, reinvested, or not yet accessible. And that is precisely where the strategy breaks down.

The problem isn't the idea. The problem is that most people try to retire a large debt with tools designed for a small one.

This is the gap that most exit planning conversations never reach. The strategy is correct. The execution is where it breaks down â and where a structured approach changes everything.
