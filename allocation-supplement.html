Inherited Plan Analysis
You indicated an inherited qualified plan in your StructureReview™. These funds must be distributed within the SECURE Act 10-year window — but rather than viewing this as a tax burden, it is a strategic bridge income asset that can reduce what you need to set aside in Layer 4.
Inherited Plan Details
SECURE Act: Most non-spouse beneficiaries must fully distribute an inherited IRA or 401(k) within 10 years. These mandatory distributions can be strategically timed to serve as bridge income — covering living expenses before other retirement income comes fully online. The timing and amount directly affect how much capital Layer 4 needs to hold.

4Stay the plan — drops like this are expected

2. In retirement, if your investments dropped and you had to spend noticeably less — fewer vacations, less dining out, cutting back on things you enjoy — how would that feel?
1That would be devastating — not acceptable
2Very stressful — I would lose sleep over it
3Uncomfortable, but I could adjust for a while
4I could manage — I've handled tough times before
5Completely fine — flexibility doesn't bother me
3. During your working years, you likely reacted differently to market swings — you had time to recover and weren't living off your savings yet. Now think about retirement, when your portfolio may be your primary income source. How do you think you will react if markets drop significantly?
Why this matters: In retirement, a major market drop doesn't just affect your account balance — it can force you to sell investments at a loss to cover living expenses. Unlike your working years, there is no paycheck to offset the decline or wait for a recovery. Be honest about how that reality would feel.
1Very worried — I'd want to protect what I have immediately
2Concerned — I'd want to reduce exposure before it gets worse
3Uneasy — but I'd try to stay patient and not overreact
4Fairly calm — I understand downturns are part of the process
5Unbothered — I have a plan and I'd stick to it regardless
4. Imagine two retirement income options. Option A pays you a fixed $5,000/month — guaranteed for life, no matter what markets do. Option B pays $4,000–$8,000/month depending on how investments perform — sometimes more, sometimes less. Which appeals to you?
1Definitely Option A — I need to know exactly what's coming
2Leaning Option A — predictability matters most
3Either works — I see trade-offs in both
4Leaning Option B — I'll take the variability for more upside
5Definitely Option B — I want the higher potential

You wouldn't write a check to the IRS to do it. However, it does require additional planning steps and works best when implemented over time. Knowing that it involves some extra effort — how motivated would you be to explore it?
Note: This question measures your openness to tax-efficient planning — not your comfort with risk. Your answer helps your advisor understand how to approach this conversation with you. It is not included in your risk score.
