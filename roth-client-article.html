Your IRA Balance
Isn't What You
Think It Is

There's a debt embedded in every traditional retirement account. Most people never settle it on their own terms — and pay dearly for it later.

By Jeffrey Faine, CLU  ·  Integrated Planning Partners  ·  integratedplanningpartners.com

Let's Start Here

The Number on Your Statement
Isn't Your Number

If you've spent your career putting money into a traditional IRA or 401(k), you've done everything right. You saved consistently. You let it grow. You deferred the taxes because that was the deal — pay them later, when you presumably earn less.

But look at that balance on your statement. That number belongs to two parties — you, and the IRS. The government has a standing claim on a significant portion of every dollar in that account. It isn't a future possibility. It isn't contingent on anything. It's a debt that already exists, embedded in the account, accruing in silence while the balance grows.

When you withdraw, you pay it. When you turn 73, Required Minimum Distributions force you to start paying it whether you're ready or not — on a schedule set by the IRS, not by you. And the rate? Congress determines that. Not you.

The balance on your statement is not what you have. It's what you have before the debt is settled.

A Roth conversion is the process of settling that debt on your own terms — paying the tax now, at today's rates, in amounts you control — so that what remains is fully and permanently yours. It grows tax-free. It comes out tax-free. It never forces a withdrawal you didn't choose.

Think of it as retiring the debt before the creditor sets the repayment schedule for you. Because eventually, one way or another, that debt gets paid. The only question is whether you control the terms.

What Actually Changes

The Difference a Roth
Makes in Real Life

The financial case for converting is compelling. But the real impact isn't on a spreadsheet — it's in how your retirement actually feels from year to year.

Picture This

You're 68. You want $8,000 for a vacation you've been planning for years. With a traditional IRA, $8,000 isn't what you withdraw — it's what you need to net after taxes. To put $8,000 in your pocket, you may need to pull $10,500 to $11,500 or more from the account, depending on your bracket. That extra $2,500 to $3,500 goes straight to the IRS. It also counts as income — potentially pushing you into a higher bracket, increasing your Medicare premiums, and causing more of your Social Security benefit to be taxed.

With a Roth IRA, you withdraw exactly $8,000. That is what hits your account. No gross-up. No income event. No Medicare surcharge. No bracket math.

Same trip. Meaningfully different cost — depending entirely on which account you're drawing from.

  • You control when you take money out — not the IRS. Required Minimum Distributions don't apply to Roth IRAs. You withdraw on your schedule, for your reasons, in the amounts you choose.

  • Everything that grows is yours — including the growth. A $200,000 conversion that becomes $400,000 over 15 years produces $200,000 of completely tax-free growth. That's not a projection — that's how Roth accounts work.

  • Your Social Security becomes more valuable. Traditional IRA withdrawals count as income and can cause up to 85% of your Social Security benefit to be taxed. Roth distributions don't count. That difference adds up significantly over a long retirement.

  • What you leave behind is cleaner. When your children or grandchildren inherit a Roth IRA, they receive it tax-free. A traditional IRA inheritance comes with a tax bill attached. Converting now is one of the most direct ways to protect what you pass on.

  • Future tax changes don't threaten you. Nobody knows what brackets will look like in 10 years. A Roth eliminates an entire category of future tax exposure. That certainty has real value — especially when you're living on a fixed income.

The Honest Truth

Why Most People
Never Finish a Conversion

Everything above is true. And yet the vast majority of people who understand it — and genuinely want to do it — never complete a Roth conversion at the scale that would actually change their retirement. There's a reason for that, and it's not a lack of willpower.

A Roth conversion doesn't make your taxes disappear. It moves them forward. You pay them this year, on the amount you convert, at ordinary income rates. That means if you have $1.5 million in a traditional IRA and you want to convert it all, you're looking at a tax bill that could easily reach $400,000 to $600,000 or more.

You can instruct your custodian to withhold that tax directly from the IRA — but doing so means a significant portion of your balance never makes it into the Roth. If you convert $1.5 million and withhold $450,000 to cover the taxes, you've converted $1.05 million to Roth and permanently forfeited $450,000 of tax-free growth potential. The money that paid the tax bill will never compound inside the Roth. It's gone, along with every dollar of future growth it would have generated.

The mathematically superior approach — and the one that actually maximizes the benefit — is to pay the tax from assets outside the IRA, so the full balance converts and compounds tax-free. For most people with $1.5 million or more in a qualified plan, that outside cash simply doesn't exist in the amounts required. And that is precisely where the strategy breaks down.

The second problem is time. The standard advice is to convert gradually — a little each year, staying within a comfortable tax bracket. It's a reasonable strategy. But for accounts over $1.5 million, the math starts working against you. Your account keeps growing while you're converting. Required Minimum Distributions kick in at 73 and force taxable income on top of whatever you're already converting. The 15-year window you thought you had turns out to be closer to 8 — and you've converted maybe 40% of what you needed to.

The problem isn't the idea. The problem is that most people try to retire a large debt with tools designed for a small one.

This is the gap that most retirement conversations never address. The strategy is correct. The execution is where it breaks down — and where a structured approach makes all the difference.

A Different Approach

What Changes When the
Tax Bill Is Financed

Remember what we established earlier: the tax on your IRA isn't a future event — it's an existing debt. The government already holds a lien on a significant portion of your retirement account. That lien doesn't disappear. It gets paid one way or another, on terms that are largely set by someone else.

With IncomeArc™, we do one better — we pay the tax from outside the IRA using the bank's money.

What IncomeArc™ does is refinance that debt. Instead of leaving the government's lien in place — accruing silently, subject to whatever tax rates Congress sets, forced into repayment on the IRS's schedule at 73 — we replace it with bank financing on terms you control. The outstanding tax obligation owed to the government is retired using the bank's capital, not yours.

And here is what most people don't expect when they hear the word "bank": there is no personal guaranty. The loan is secured solely by the life insurance policy itself. Your home, your savings, your other accounts — none of them are on the hook. The liability hasn't changed in size since it was owed to the government. But what changed are two things that matter enormously: the timeline, and the rate.

The government's effective rate on this debt is your marginal income tax bracket — 24%, 32%, 37%, or higher, depending on your situation, and subject to increase any time Congress chooses. The bank's rate is approximately 5% to 6%. That is the refinancing. You are trading a 24% to 40% obligation on the government's repayment schedule for a 5% to 6% obligation on yours. In any other context — a mortgage, a business loan, a line of credit — that would be an obvious decision. Applied to the largest debt most retirees are carrying without knowing it, it changes the entire financial picture of retirement.

Your interest rate will never be as high as your tax rate. And your tax rate will never be as low as your interest rate.

But the refinancing doesn't stop with the bank. As the policy accumulates cash value over time, the next step is to refinance again — this time replacing the bank loan with a policy loan from the insurance company. This is simply changing the lender one more time. The bank is paid off. The insurance company becomes the new creditor. And the rate on a policy loan is typically even more favorable than the bank's.

This step matters for a specific reason: the bank must be repaid before you can begin drawing tax-free income from the policy. Once the bank is retired and the policy loan is in place, the cash value inside the IUL becomes accessible as tax-free income — drawn against the policy's accumulated value without triggering a taxable event. That is the income the policy was built to produce.

From that point forward, the remaining policy loan — now held by the insurance company, not the bank — simply rides. There is no required repayment schedule. No personal guaranty. No forced distribution. When you pass, the gross death benefit repays the outstanding policy loan in full. What remains — the net death benefit — passes to your beneficiaries completely tax-free.

Consider what that means in contrast to the alternative. An inherited traditional IRA or qualified retirement plan comes with the full pre-tax tax liability attached. Your beneficiaries are required to draw it down within ten years and pay ordinary income tax on every dollar. What you leave them is not a gift — it is a tax bill with your name on it. What the IncomeArc™ structure leaves them is a tax-free inheritance, after a debt that began as a government lien quietly settled itself from within the policy.

Here's what that makes possible. The bank financing activates both layers of the IncomeArc™ structure — and while each layer does its own independent job, they are designed to work together for a better combined outcome than either produces alone:

Layer 3 — Tax Deferral and long-term growth. The distribution proceeds fund an Indexed Universal Life policy — accumulating cash value with tax-deferred growth, protected from market downside, and accessible tax-free. The bank loan services the tax obligation on the distribution. Your personal savings stay intact. Over time, the IUL grows, the bank is refinanced through a policy loan, and the cash value becomes the source of additional tax-free income from Layer 3.

Layer 1 — The Guaranteed Income Foundation. The converted Roth balance funds a guaranteed annuity — contractual, permanent, completely tax-free income for life. This is Layer 1, and it can be funded independently of Layer 3. What Layer 3 does is remove the cashflow friction that limits how much gets funded. Without the leverage component, a significant portion of the conversion is consumed by the tax bill. With it, far more of the converted balance reaches Layer 1 intact — which means a larger, more complete guaranteed income foundation.

This is the competitive advantage of the IncomeArc™ system. The architecture itself — four layers, each doing a distinct job — is more complete than what most retirement planning delivers. But what takes it to the next level is the leverage: bringing bank financing into the structure removes the cashflow drag of taxes and allows the system to work at a scale that conventional planning simply cannot reach. Layer 1 is designed to target your Life Rate™ as the income foundation. Layer 3 is what allows it to do so more fully.
